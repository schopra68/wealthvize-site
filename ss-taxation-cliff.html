The Inherited IRA Tax Bomb: What Your $2M IRA Actually Leaves Your Kids

You've spent 30 years building a $2 million IRA. You've been thoughtful about taxes, done Roth conversions in the right years, planned your distributions carefully. You plan to leave it to your children as a cornerstone of their financial security.

Here's what they'll actually receive under current law: somewhere between $1.1 million and $1.4 million, after taxes. The rest — $600,000 to $900,000 — goes to the IRS, collected at your children's tax bracket, not yours.

What SECURE Act 2.0 changed

Before 2020, non-spouse beneficiaries could "stretch" inherited IRA distributions over their own life expectancy — sometimes 40 or 50 years. The tax was spread across decades. The account compounded tax-deferred for a generation.

SECURE Act 2.0 eliminated the stretch for most non-spouse beneficiaries. Your children must now withdraw the entire inherited IRA balance within 10 years of your death. No exceptions based on account size. No exceptions for high-bracket beneficiaries.

The real math of forced depletion

Assume you leave your child a $2 million inherited IRA. They're 45 years old, earning $200,000 from their career. They must withdraw approximately $200,000 per year for 10 years.

Adding $200,000 of IRA income to their $200,000 salary puts them at $400,000 of income — solidly in the 35% federal bracket, potentially the 37% bracket depending on deductions and state.

Inherited IRA balance$2,000,000
Required annual withdrawal (10 years)~$200,000/yr
Federal income tax (35% bracket)$70,000/yr
State income tax (4.4% — Colorado)$8,800/yr
Total tax over 10 years~$788,000
Net your child actually receives~$1,212,000

Forty percent of the account — nearly $800,000 — goes to taxes. The "gift" you carefully built becomes a forced tax event at the worst possible moment in your child's financial life.

Why Roth conversions now are the most powerful estate planning tool you have

The fix is straightforward in concept: convert pre-tax IRA dollars to Roth before you die. Here's why it's so valuable specifically for your heirs:

You pay the conversion tax at your rate (likely 22–24%). Your children would have paid it at their rate (32–37%). The spread between those rates — up to 15 percentage points — is pure generational tax savings. And the Roth grows tax-free in the meantime, potentially doubling or tripling before they inherit it.

The conversion math: Converting $500,000 from pre-tax to Roth at your 24% bracket costs $120,000 in conversion taxes. If your child would have paid 35% on that same $500,000, their tax would have been $175,000. Your conversion saves your heirs $55,000 on that $500,000 alone — and the Roth grows tax-free until they inherit it. If it doubles to $1M before inheritance, the tax saving on that growth is additional.

Inherited Roth: a completely different outcome

Non-spouse heirs still must deplete an inherited Roth within 10 years — that part of SECURE Act didn't change. But the withdrawals are completely tax-free. Your child withdraws $200,000 per year from the inherited Roth for 10 years and owes nothing. The entire $2 million transfers intact. The generational wealth you built is preserved dollar-for-dollar.

Where your heirs live matters enormously

If your children live in a no-income-tax state — Texas, Florida, Nevada, Wyoming — they avoid state tax on every inherited IRA withdrawal. On a $2 million inherited IRA, that's $88,000 in state tax savings over 10 years compared to a Colorado heir, and over $250,000 compared to a California heir.

This is a planning variable most estate attorneys never raise. It's real money — and it changes the calculus of Roth conversion vs leaving pre-tax depending entirely on where your children live and work.

The question that changes your conversion strategy

Most Roth conversion decisions focus entirely on your own tax situation: "What bracket am I in today? What will my RMDs be at 73?" These are the right questions for personal tax planning.

But for multigenerational wealth transfer, the more important question is: "What bracket will my children be in during the 10 years after I die?"

If the answer is 32–37% — which is typical for children in their 45–55 peak earning years — then every dollar you convert at 24% today saves your heirs 8–13 cents on the dollar. On a $5 million pre-tax account, that's $400,000–$650,000 in generational tax savings from a systematic conversion strategy.

A note on state estate taxes

Colorado has no state estate tax — one advantage of staying in-state. But if your heirs move to Washington, Oregon, or Massachusetts, they may face a state estate tax on the inherited assets in addition to federal income tax on IRA withdrawals. This stacks badly for large inherited IRAs in high-estate-tax states and is another argument for converting to Roth before death, since Roth assets are fully included in estate value but at least avoid the income tax layer on withdrawal.

The inherited IRA is one of the most complex planning problems in retirement — touching income tax, estate tax, generational timing, and state law simultaneously. But the core insight is simple: the taxes your heirs pay on your IRA are negotiated by the decisions you make today.
